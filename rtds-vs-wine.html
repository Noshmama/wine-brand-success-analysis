Do RTDs Compete Directly with Wine?

Below $10, yes — value wine is losing casual occasions to simpler, single-serve alternatives. Above $15, no — nobody is choosing between a Cabernet and a can.

The Short Answer

At the Value Tier, Yes.
At Premium and Above, No.

Below $10, value wine is losing casual-drinking occasions to simpler, single-serve RTDs. Above $15, RTDs are irrelevant — nobody is choosing between a $22 Cabernet and a can of anything. The competitive threat depends entirely on price tier.

Why the Confusion?

The Legal Classification Problem

Products like BeatBox (12M cases, acquired by AB InBev for $490M) and Vibe Twisted Sips (Gallo, +42.2%) are legally classified as "wine-based" because of their production base. This leads industry reports to count them as wine brands, inflating apparent wine category growth and distorting the picture of what drives actual wine brand success.

But the consumer buying a BeatBox Tetra Pak at a music festival is not making a wine purchase decision. They are choosing between BeatBox, White Claw, High Noon, and beer — not between BeatBox and Chardonnay. The legal classification and the consumer reality are two entirely different things.

Three Types of RTDs, Three Different Competitive Sets

Spirits-Based RTDs

e.g., High Noon, Surfside, Jack & Coke cans
  • Made with real vodka, tequila, or whiskey
  • Positioned as cocktails in a can
  • Sold in beer coolers and c-stores
  • $2.50-4.00 per can
  • Occasion: casual social, outdoor, sports
Competes With
Beer, hard seltzers, malt-based seltzers. Not wine.

Wine-Based RTDs

e.g., BeatBox, Vibe Twisted Sips
  • Wine base but heavily flavored/sweetened
  • Tetra Paks, cans, single-serve
  • Sold alongside seltzers and beer
  • $3.00-5.00 per unit
  • Occasion: festivals, parties, on-the-go
Competes With
Hard seltzers, spirits-based RTDs, flavored malt beverages. Not wine.

Traditional Wine

e.g., Josh Cellars, La Marca, Caymus
  • Grape-based, varietal or region-identified
  • 750ml bottles, premium boxes
  • Sold in wine aisle or wine shops
  • $8-100+ per bottle
  • Occasion: dinner, entertaining, gifting
Competes With
Under $10: loses casual occasions to RTDs, beer, and seltzers on convenience. Above $15: competes only with other wines. RTDs are irrelevant at this tier.

Where RTDs Do Compete with Wine: The Value Tier

Below $10, Wine Is Losing Casual Occasions

A consumer deciding between an $8 bottle of Sutter Home and a $3.50 can of High Noon for a casual Tuesday evening is increasingly reaching for the can — not because it's better than wine, but because it's simpler and single-serve. No commitment, no corkscrew, no leftover half-bottle.

This is not a quality comparison. It's a convenience and occasion comparison. The value wine brands declining fastest — Woodbridge (-9%), Sutter Home (-3.5%), Livingston (-5%) — are losing to a shift in casual-drinking habits where simpler, single-serve options win the moment.

Where RTDs Are Irrelevant: Premium and Above

Above $15, Nobody Is Choosing Between Wine and a Can

Nobody choosing between a $22 DAOU Cabernet and a $50 bottle of Caymus is considering High Noon as an alternative. The occasions are completely different: dinner parties, celebrations, wine collecting, gifting, restaurant dining. RTDs simply don't exist in this world.

This is why premium wine brands (Josh Cellars, DAOU, Decoy, Kim Crawford, La Marca) are growing or stable while value brands decline. They occupy occasions that RTDs cannot reach.

The Bigger Picture: Beverage-Agnostic Consumers

Younger Drinkers Pick by Occasion, Not Category

Millennials and Gen Z (now 45% of wine drinkers) are beverage-agnostic. They don't identify as "wine drinkers" or "spirits drinkers" — they choose from a wider repertoire based on the occasion.

A Millennial hosting dinner might serve Josh Cellars. The same person at a tailgate grabs High Noon. At brunch, it's Prosecco. Watching a game at home, it's beer. This means wine's challenge isn't a direct fight with RTDs — it's competing for a share of occasions in a more fragmented landscape than it faced a decade ago.

Who Actually Competes with Whom: By Occasion

What This Means for Wine Brand Strategy

  • Don't try to become an RTD. Wine's strength is in occasions RTDs can't reach: dinner, entertaining, gifting, restaurant dining, collecting. Lean into these.
  • Expand wine's occasion set with format innovation. Black Box (+23%), Bota Box Breeze (+36%), and Decoy Featherweight (+220%) succeed by making wine work in more casual moments — not by imitating RTDs.
  • Single-serve is about convenience, not competition. Barefoot's 200ml Tetra Paks and canned rosés make wine accessible for outdoor and on-the-go occasions. This expands wine's territory rather than defending against RTDs.
  • Premium wine is insulated. Brands priced above $15 face virtually no RTD competition. The growth strategy at this tier is about quality, story, and consistency — not format wars.
  • Value wine's real problem is value wine. Sub-$10 brands are declining because of thin margins, aging consumers, retailer deauthorization, and lack of brand investment — not because of RTDs specifically. RTDs are a symptom of the broader shift, not the cause.
  • Recruit beverage-agnostic consumers for wine occasions. The opportunity is not to fight RTDs for the tailgate. It's to make wine relevant to the 45% of drinkers under 45 who are open to wine but need a reason to choose it for their next dinner, date, or gathering.
